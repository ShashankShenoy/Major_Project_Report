\chapter{Literature Review and Theoretical Background}

Before delving into the technical architecture of the MARVIS system, it is crucial to understand the existing landscape of maritime surveillance technologies. The transition from manual watch-standing to automated, vision-driven systems has generated a massive body of research over the past decade. This chapter provides a comprehensive review of the theoretical foundations and recent advancements driving this field. Specifically, it examines the evolution of deep learning applied to ship detection, the complexities of maintaining object identities through multi-target tracking, and the predictive algorithms used to forecast vessel trajectories based on historical \gls{ais} data.

\section{Introduction}
The rapid progression of computer vision has fundamentally altered maritime surveillance. Traditional methods relied heavily on manual observation or purely radar-based detection, both of which struggle with non-cooperative vessels and dense coastal traffic. Recently, deep learning has offered a pathway to fully automated monitoring. This chapter surveys the current landscape of maritime surveillance technologies, focusing on three core areas: ship detection, multi-object tracking, and trajectory prediction. By examining these fields, we identify the specific limitations in current approaches that motivate the integrated pipeline proposed in this project.

\section{Deep Learning-Based Ship Detection}
Optical and Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery have both been used extensively to locate vessels at sea. A dominant trend in recent literature is the adaptation of the YOLO architecture---particularly YOLOv8---to handle the unique challenges of maritime environments, such as variable target sizes, occlusion, and difficult lighting conditions.

\subsection{Architectural Adaptations for Maritime Environments}
Standard object detectors often struggle with the extreme scale variations seen in maritime imagery. For instance, a small fishing boat captured from a high-altitude drone looks vastly different from a cargo ship viewed near the coast. To address this, researchers have proposed various multi-scale feature extraction strategies. \cite{Liu2025} introduced a bidirectional feature pyramid for YOLOv8 that improves the flow of semantic information across different scales, which proved particularly effective for high-resolution remote sensing. Similarly, \cite{Shi2025} integrated a Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) into the YOLO backbone. This attention mechanism suppresses irrelevant sea-clutter while amplifying the features of small ships, a concept that is highly relevant when detecting distant vessels in our drone-based setup.

Further pushing the boundaries of small-target detection, \cite{Jiang2024} developed YOLOv8-FDF specifically designed to isolate tiny vessels amidst the overwhelming visual noise of complex coastal scenes. We see a parallel approach by \cite{Yang2024}, who successfully combined bespoke feature enhancement layers with spatial attention mechanisms to dramatically reduce false positives in radar imagery. Another major challenge is target orientation. Vessels in satellite or drone imagery appear at arbitrary angles, making standard bounding boxes less precise. \cite{Ning2024} tackled this by developing an oriented bounding-box (OBB) detection head for SAR imagery, significantly reducing background interference. While SAR data presents unique coherent-imaging characteristics that require specialised preprocessing \cite{Yu2023}, the underlying need for precise localisation remains constant across modalities. We see similar structural adaptations in \cite{Fu2025}, where deformable convolutions were added to YOLOv8 to better capture the irregular silhouettes of ships under partial occlusion or in infrared imagery \cite{Chen2024}. To fully capitalise on spatial relationships across consecutive frames, \cite{Wu2025} even introduced a complex two-stage architecture marrying a space-to-depth YOLOv8 with a Vision Transformer (ViT), proving that temporal context vastly improves baseline detection accuracy.

\subsection{Efficiency and Real-Time Deployment}
Because maritime surveillance systems often operate on edge devices with limited power, balancing detection accuracy with inference speed is critical. \cite{Gao2024} developed SM-LightNet, a lightweight variant of YOLOv8 that uses depthwise separable convolutions to maintain real-time performance on mobile GPUs. A comparative study by \cite{Liu2025b} evaluated different YOLOv8 scales and concluded that while the 'nano' variant is highly efficient, the 'medium' variant offers the best trade-off for complex maritime scenes where precision on small targets is required. This directly informed our choice to deploy YOLOv8m as the primary detection backbone. Similarly, \cite{Xu2025} proposed RLE-YOLO, an ultra-lightweight multiscale framework explicitly built to run on constrained satellite hardware without sacrificing recall rates.

Furthermore, \cite{Chen2023} demonstrated that optimising post-processing steps, such as dynamic non-maximum suppression (NMS), can reduce latency by up to 12\% without sacrificing accuracy. For systems needing to observe massive ocean expanses, \cite{Gong2024} showed that super-resolution branches could recover detection capability for extremely distant targets, and \cite{Huang2024} proved that training on multi-source data yields a more robust feature extractor. Finally, foundational benchmarking by \cite{Nambiar2022} established that while SAR is superior in fog or at night, optical video remains the most reliable modality for clear daytime operation, justifying our focus on optical streams.

\section{Multi-Object Tracking and Re-Identification}
Detecting a ship is only the first step; maintaining its identity across frames is what enables traffic analysis and threat assessment. The Simple Online and Real-time Tracking (SORT) algorithm and its deep-learning extensions have become the standard for this task.

\subsection{Robust Tracking Under Occlusion}
In busy ports, ships frequently obscure one another. \cite{Belmouhcine2021} noted that standard DeepSORT is brittle to these partial occlusions, often dropping tracks or switching identities. They proposed a robust distance metric that downweights noisy detections during occlusion, significantly reducing identity switches. They later extended this work \cite{Belmouhcine2022} by replacing hand-crafted metric learning with an attention-based re-identification (ReID) module, which computed similarity scores much more efficiently. This evolution toward learned appearance matching heavily influenced our use of the OSNet ReID module to handle vessel tracking through visual interruptions.

Building upon these foundations, \cite{Zang2024} seamlessly integrated YOLOv8 with DeepSORT to drastically improve continuity during dense, chaotic scenarios involving sudden smoke or weather obscurations. Their findings mirror the work of \cite{Zhu2024}, who originally proved the viability of YOLO-driven multi-target surface tracking for naval applications. When dealing with specialized operations, assigning tracking weights becomes crucial. For example, \cite{Nithya2023} designed a prioritized tracking framework for navy operations, ensuring that smaller, high-risk targets are aggressively tracked even if they cross paths with massive commercial vessels. Recent studies have also explored incorporating domain-specific constraints to further improve tracking. \cite{Muslim2025} developed a port-specific tracking system that used geometric priors about berth locations to reject physically impossible vessel movements, cutting down on false positives caused by wave reflections. Meanwhile, \cite{Chen2025} focused entirely on resolving identity switches during severe occlusion events by retaining a longer temporal memory of vessel features. 

\subsection{Large-Scale and Multi-Sensor Tracking}
As surveillance networks grow, trackers must handle data from multiple sources. \cite{Liu2024} addressed the complex problem of tracking ships across multiple satellites, highlighting the need for robust spatial hand-offs. On a local scale, \cite{Guan2023} explored multi-sensor tracking frameworks that fuse data from overlapping coastal cameras. Interestingly, \cite{Wan2025} took this a step further by outlining a comprehensive multi-source sensing fusion architecture explicitly tailored to handle dynamic, rapidly shifting maritime weather conditions. \cite{Wang2025a} applied a YOLO-DeepSORT pipeline to monitor traffic flow in a busy shipping lane, successfully extracting speed distributions and lane occupancy statistics. \cite{Suliva2022} provided a comprehensive benchmark of visual multi-object trackers in maritime scenarios, concluding that appearance-augmented SORT variants offer the best balance of speed and reliability for real-time applications.

\section{Trajectory Prediction and AIS Data Fusion}
The final component of our proposed system is predicting where a vessel will go next. This requires analysing historical movement data, often derived from Automatic Identification System (AIS) broadcasts.

\subsection{Sequence Modeling for Trajectories}
Vessel movement is inherently sequential, making Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks popular choices for trajectory prediction. Early work by \cite{Urakami2018} demonstrated the feasibility of using basic neural networks to forecast ship paths from AIS data. \cite{Capobianco2021} subsequently applied RNNs to model the non-linear dynamics of ship maneuvers in restricted waters. \cite{Yang2022} framed the problem as a sequence-to-sequence translation task, using encoder-decoder architectures to map past AIS positions to future coordinate sequences. 

The integration of contextual data has further refined these models. \cite{Chondrodima2023} highlighted the importance of preprocessing and fusing raw AIS data, which is notoriously noisy and irregularly sampled, before feeding it into deep learning models. \cite{Zhao2024} incorporated spatio-temporal patterns to capture the influence of ocean currents and shipping lanes on vessel behavior. Pushing contextual awareness to the extreme, \cite{Zhang2024} introduced a multi-stage dynamic approach that deeply integrates the surrounding navigational context, adjusting its predictions dynamically as ships encounter varying local traffic densities. Similarly, \cite{Zygouras2024} developed an environmental clustering methodology that extracts common historical pathways, effectively anchoring the network's predictions to known, safe maritime corridors. Furthermore, \cite{Liu2022} demonstrated that LSTMs are highly effective at flagging anomalous trajectories that deviate from established historical routes. 

\subsection{Advanced Prediction Architectures}
More recently, researchers have moved beyond standard LSTMs. \cite{Wang2024a} introduced a Trajectory Transformer that leverages self-attention to capture long-range dependencies in vessel paths, outperforming recurrent models on long-term forecasting tasks. We see exactly this trend continuing with \cite{Nguyen2024}, who utilised a Sparse Augmented Data Representation Transformer to gracefully handle the frequent data gaps inherent to commercial AIS broadcasts. Taking a more holistic view, \cite{Zhang2026a} proposed a unified multimodal architecture that goes beyond simple path generation; it attempts to output an explainable navigational intention, allowing human operators to understand exactly why the AI predicted a specific maneuver.

\cite{Zang2024} also utilised attention mechanisms to selectively weight historical data points based on their relevance to the current maneuver. Finally, \cite{Aouedi2026} provided a modern LSTM-based framework tailored for real-time forecasting, which balances computational overhead with predictive accuracy. These advancements validate our decision to employ an LSTM-based forecasting module that uses a dynamically scaled prediction window based on real-time vessel speed.

\section{Summary}
The literature demonstrates clear progress in maritime surveillance, moving from basic detection to robust, multi-object tracking and predictive modeling. However, a significant gap remains: most studies isolate these tasks. Detection models are evaluated on static datasets, trackers are tested without regard for long-term forecasting, and prediction models rely almost entirely on AIS data without visual corroboration. Furthermore, \cite{Zhang2026} established a critical link between vision-based detection and AIS records, underscoring the value of multimodal data fusion. This project directly addresses that gap by integrating YOLOv8-based detection, deep appearance tracking, and LSTM trajectory prediction into a single, cohesive system that fuses visual tracking with AIS data for real-time maritime domain awareness.
