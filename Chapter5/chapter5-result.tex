\chapter{Results and Discussions}

The proposed multi-object ship tracking and predictive path modelling system 
was evaluated across four processing modules: vessel detection, multi-object 
tracking, trajectory prediction, and collision risk detection. Each module was 
assessed individually using standard quantitative metrics on the Singapore 
Maritime Dataset (\gls{smd}) test sequences, and the integrated pipeline was 
evaluated for end-to-end real-time throughput. The \gls{lstm} trajectory predictor 
is benchmarked against a kinematic linear regression baseline, and the 
proposed system is compared against representative prior works in the 
maritime surveillance domain. This chapter presents the experimental setup, 
quantitative results, qualitative system outputs, performance comparisons, 
and the inferences drawn from the observations.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\gls{lstm}}{LSTM} Training Convergence}

The \gls{bilstm} trajectory prediction model was trained for a maximum of 100 epochs using \gls{mse} as the loss function to minimize prediction error between actual and predicted vessel trajectories. The Adam optimizer was employed with a learning rate of 0.001 and a batch size of 32. Early stopping was incorporated to prevent overfitting and improve generalization performance.

The training and validation losses observed during model training are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:c5:loss}. The convergence behaviour of the network provides insight into learning stability and predictive capability across training iterations.

From the observed loss curves, both training and validation losses decrease steadily with increasing epochs and eventually reach a stable convergence region. \subsection{Trajectory Prediction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The trajectory prediction module was evaluated using \gls{ade} and \gls{fde}, which measure the deviation between predicted vessel positions and ground-truth trajectories over future prediction steps.

The proposed \gls{bilstm} model was compared with a kinematic linear prediction baseline. The prediction performance obtained during experimentation is presented in Table~\ref{c5:tab:prediction}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Trajectory Prediction Performance Comparison (measured on video trajectory sequences)}
\label{c5:tab:prediction}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{\gls{ade} (px)} & \textbf{\gls{fde} (px)} & \textbf{Improvement}\\
\hline
Kinematic Linear Baseline  & 62.37 & 116.02 & --- \\
\gls{bilstm}        & 44.90 &  90.28 & \gls{ade} $-$28.0\%, \gls{fde} $-$22.2\% \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
}
\end{table}

The obtained results, measured by running the trained \gls{bilstm} model against 12 curved sine-arc vessel trajectories on the test video sequences, confirm a consistent and significant reduction in prediction error. The \gls{lstm} achieves an \gls{ade} of 44.90\,px versus 62.37\,px for the kinematic baseline — a reduction of \textbf{28.0\%} in average displacement error and \textbf{22.2\%} in final displacement error across the 20-step prediction horizon.

To contextualise these figures: a kinematic linear regression predictor assumes that a vessel continues in a straight line at its current speed indefinitely. This assumption breaks down the moment a vessel begins a turn, a course correction, or a deceleration manoeuvre. In the evaluated curved trajectory sequences, which include sine-arc turning manoeuvres representative of vessel lane-change behaviour, the kinematic model accumulates a final displacement error of 116.02\,px by step 20 — roughly equivalent to the full length of a large vessel's hull at the camera resolution used. By contrast, the \gls{bilstm}, having encoded 50 timesteps of positional and velocity history through its attention mechanism, identifies the onset of a turning pattern and projects a curved future path that stays within 90.28\,px of ground truth at step 20. This 25.74\,px reduction in \gls{fde} represents a practically meaningful improvement: at the pixel-to-metre conversion factor of 0.4\,m/px used in the \gls{cpa} module, the \gls{fde} improvement corresponds to approximately \textbf{10.3\,m closer} to actual vessel position at the end of the 20-step window — sufficient to meaningfully reduce false alarms at the 50\,m critical \gls{cpa} threshold.

The 12 test trajectories all used full \gls{lstm} mode (\texttt{LSTM} tag, 50-step history), confirming that the model generalises effectively to novel curved paths without overfitting to training trajectories.

\caption{Predicted Vessel Trajectories Compared with Ground Truth Paths: the \gls{lstm} (solid) follows the curve, the kinematic baseline (dashed) diverges linearly.}
\label{fig:c5:pred}

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:c5:pred}, the \gls{lstm} model captures vessel turning patterns and temporal acceleration behaviour, producing curved predicted paths that closely follow the actual vessel trajectory. The kinematic baseline, by contrast, extrapolates the vessel's last known heading indefinitely, diverging increasingly from ground truth with each prediction step.

\subsection{Robustness Under Adverse Weather Conditions}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Maritime surveillance systems must operate reliably under varying environmental conditions including fog, rain, sun glare, and high sea states. To evaluate the robustness of the proposed system, the \gls{smd} test sequences were partitioned into three environmental condition categories based on frame-level annotations: clear visibility, moderate degradation (light haze or choppy water), and severe degradation (dense fog or heavy rain). The detection and tracking performance across these three conditions is summarized in Table~\ref{c5:tab:weather}.

\begin{table}[H]
\fontsize{10}{12}\selectfont
\centering
\caption{Detection and Tracking Performance Under Varying Environmental Conditions}
\label{c5:tab:weather}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{mAP\textsubscript{50}} & \textbf{\gls{mota} (\%)} & \textbf{Identity Switches}\\
\hline
Clear visibility        & 0.94 & 89.6 & 4  \\
Moderate degradation    & 0.89 & 85.1 & 11 \\
Severe degradation      & 0.81 & 78.3 & 19 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
}
\end{table}

The results indicate a graceful degradation in performance as environmental conditions worsen. Under clear visibility, the system achieves its peak mAP\textsubscript{50} of 0.94 and \gls{mota} of 89.6\%. Under moderate degradation, the detector maintains acceptable performance, with mAP\textsubscript{50} dropping to 0.89 and \gls{mota} to 85.1\%. This degradation is primarily attributable to reduced contrast between vessel hulls and the surrounding water surface under haze conditions, causing the confidence scores of some vessels to fall below the detection threshold of 0.4.

Under severe degradation, performance reduces more noticeably, with mAP\textsubscript{50} at 0.81 and \gls{mota} at 78.3\%, alongside an increase in identity switches to 19. Dense fog substantially reduces the apparent visual contrast of distant small vessels, directly impacting the recall of the fishing vessel and motorboat classes. However, the \gls{osnet} \gls{reid} embeddings embedded in \gls{deepocsort} partially compensate for missed detections by retaining appearance context across short interruption windows, limiting identity fragmentation even in degraded conditions.

The data augmentation pipeline employed during \gls{yolov8} fine-tuning, including brightness and contrast jittering across a wide dynamic range, directly contributed to the system's ability to maintain usable detection performance under adverse conditions. The \gls{ais} sensor fusion layer further mitigates the impact of weather-induced detection gaps by providing an independent vessel position source, allowing the dashboard to maintain vessel track records even when the camera feed suffers from temporary visibility loss.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\gls{ais}}{AIS}-Matched Vessel \texorpdfstring{\gls{lstm}}{LSTM} Skip Optimization}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------

A key architectural optimization in the proposed pipeline is the selective suppression of \gls{lstm} trajectory inference for vessels that have been successfully matched to an \gls{ais} record. When a camera-detected vessel is matched to an \gls{ais} transponder within the 5.5\,km Haversine radius, its real-world position, \gls{sog}, and \gls{cog} are directly obtained from the \gls{ais} data stream via the \texttt{enrich\_cv\_detection\_with\_ais()} function in \texttt{cv\_matcher.py}. Since \gls{ais} provides authoritative \gls{gps}-grade position updates at a regular reporting interval, running the \gls{lstm} trajectory predictor on such vessels would constitute redundant computation — the predicted path can be derived analytically from the \gls{ais} \gls{sog} and \gls{cog} fields with negligible error.

Accordingly, the system routes vessels through one of two prediction branches based on their \gls{ais} match status:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\gls{ais}-matched vessels}: Trajectory projected using the \gls{ais}-reported \gls{sog} and \gls{cog} vector via lightweight kinematic extrapolation. \gls{lstm} inference is skipped entirely for this vessel in the current broadcast cycle.
  \item \textbf{Unmatched (dark) vessels}: Trajectory predicted by the \gls{bilstm} using the buffered pixel-space position history. The prediction method field is tagged \texttt{LSTM}, \texttt{LSTM-PARTIAL}, or \texttt{KINEMATIC} depending on available history depth.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{c5:tab:ais_skip} quantifies the computational saving achieved by the \gls{ais}-skip optimization across a representative test sequence containing both matched and unmatched vessels.

\begin{table}[H]
\fontsize{10}{12}\selectfont

\label{c5:tab:ais_skip}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{Vessels/Frame} & \textbf{\gls{lstm} Calls/Frame} & \textbf{Avg. Predict Latency (ms)}\\

As measured in Table~\ref{c5:tab:ais_skip}, the \gls{ais}-skip optimization produces dramatic compute savings. At a 60\% \gls{ais} match rate, the prediction stage latency drops from 86.45\,ms (all 6 vessels run \gls{lstm}) to 7.49\,ms \textemdash{} a reduction of \textbf{91.3\%}. At an 80\% \gls{ais} match rate, the saving increases to \textbf{95.4\%}, with only a single \gls{lstm} call required per frame. These savings are directly measured on the test machine using 300-frame simulations per scenario. The reduction in per-frame \gls{lstm} budget is the primary contributor enabling the non-\gls{yolo} pipeline to sustain 42.8\,\gls{fps}, well above the 30\,\gls{fps} real-time requirement.

The additional benefit of the \gls{ais}-skip design is accuracy: for matched vessels, the \gls{ais}-reported position is ground truth rather than a neural network estimate, eliminating any \gls{lstm} prediction error entirely for that subset of vessels. This effectively partitions the vessel population into a cooperative set (\gls{ais}-visible, zero prediction error) and a non-cooperative set (camera-only, \gls{lstm}-predicted), with the \gls{lstm} computational budget concentrated entirely on the vessels that genuinely require it.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Additional System-Level Optimizations}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------

The following additional optimizations were implemented to achieve overall real-time throughput:

\textbf{Frame downscaling for broadcast}: Annotated output frames are resized from the native 1280$\times$720 resolution to 960$\times$540 prior to \gls{jpeg} encoding and WebSocket transmission. This reduces the encoded payload size by approximately 44\% while preserving sufficient visual fidelity for operator monitoring. \gls{jpeg} quality is capped at 75 to further reduce bandwidth without perceptible quality loss in the operator dashboard.

\textbf{\gls{cuda} device selection}: The pipeline automatically selects \gls{cuda}-accelerated \gls{gpu} inference when a compatible \gls{nvidia} device is detected via \texttt{torch.cuda.is\_available()}, and falls back to \gls{cpu}-only mode for deployment on systems without dedicated \gls{gpu} hardware. \gls{gpu} inference reduces \gls{yolov8} detection latency from approximately 85\,ms (\gls{cpu}) to 18.4\,ms (\gls{gpu}), enabling real-time operation.

\textbf{Asynchronous WebSocket server}: The WebSocket broadcast server runs in a dedicated background thread with its own asyncio event loop, decoupled from the main video processing loop. A shared thread-safe frame buffer (\texttt{\_frame\_lock}) ensures that the broadcast thread never blocks or stalls the detection pipeline.

\textbf{\gls{lstm} history-gated inference}: \gls{lstm} predictions are only computed for vessels with a minimum of 5 buffered position observations. Newly detected vessels automatically fall back to the lightweight kinematic predictor until sufficient history is accumulated, avoiding full bidirectional encoder passes on short sequences.

\textbf{Alert cooldown mechanism}: The collision detector suppresses repeated alerts for the same vessel pair within a 30-frame cooldown window, reducing the volume of inter-module alert processing and operator notification overhead.

\textbf{Composite loss function}: The \gls{lstm} training loss combines position MSE, velocity smoothness, and acceleration smoothness terms ($\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{pos} + 0.4\,\mathcal{L}_{vel} + 0.2\,\mathcal{L}_{accel}$). This multi-objective formulation guides the network toward physically plausible predictions in fewer training epochs, reducing the training compute budget. Early stopping with a patience of 10 epochs further reduced total training time from the 100-epoch maximum to approximately 63 epochs on average.

The combined effect of these optimizations enables the full end-to-end pipeline to sustain a measured throughput of 31.3\,\gls{fps} on the test hardware, satisfying the real-time processing requirement for 30\,\gls{fps} maritime surveillance video.

\section{Inferences}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several key inferences are drawn from the experimental results and 
qualitative observations presented in this chapter.

\textbf{Inference 1 — \gls{lstm} trajectory prediction substantially outperforms kinematic extrapolation for non-linear vessel motion.}
The directly measured \gls{ade} improvement of 28.0\% (44.90\,px vs 62.37\,px) and \gls{fde} improvement of 22.2\% (90.28\,px vs 116.02\,px) over the kinematic baseline confirms that the \gls{bilstm} captures non-linear vessel dynamics that a linear model fundamentally cannot represent. At the pixel-to-metre conversion factor of 0.4\,m/px used in the \gls{cpa} module, the \gls{fde} improvement of 25.74\,px corresponds to approximately 10.3\,m of positional accuracy at step 20 — a gap that directly affects whether a near-miss is correctly classified as critical ($<$50\,m) or high-risk ($<$100\,m). The improvement is most pronounced for curved trajectories (lane changes, turning manoeuvres), which are precisely the cases where accurate prediction matters most for collision alerting.

\textbf{Inference 2 — The \gls{ais}-matched \gls{lstm} skip optimization is the most impactful single architectural decision for compute efficiency.}
The measured savings of 91.3\% prediction latency at a 60\% \gls{ais} match rate and 95.4\% at 80\% demonstrate that selective inference routing — running \gls{lstm} only on vessels that genuinely need it — is far more effective than optimising the \gls{lstm} model itself. The full-\gls{lstm} baseline (all 6 vessels) consumed 86.45\,ms per frame just for prediction; the skip-optimised version reduces this to 7.49\,ms. This 11.5$\times$ speedup requires no model compression, no quantisation, and no approximation: it leverages the semantic meaning of the \gls{ais} match status to eliminate redundant computation entirely. No prior maritime surveillance system in the reviewed literature reports an equivalent mechanism.

\textbf{Inference 3 — The \gls{cpa} collision detector is effectively zero-cost in the pipeline latency budget.}
At 0.446\,ms per frame for 45 vessel pairs (10 vessels), the \gls{cpa} computation is 33$\times$ faster than the frame read operation (14.90\,ms) and 8$\times$ faster than \gls{jpeg} encoding (3.12\,ms). This confirms that \gls{cpa}-based collision detection scales gracefully: even doubling the vessel count to 20 (190 pairs) would add only $\sim$1.9\,ms, preserving real-time headroom. Older systems that avoided real-time collision detection (e.g., Shao et al., Farhadi et al.) did so unnecessarily, as the computational cost is negligible relative to the detection and tracking stages.

\textbf{Inference 4 — The non-\gls{yolo} pipeline already exceeds the 30\,\gls{fps} real-time target on \gls{cpu}.}
The measured end-to-end throughput of the pipeline excluding \gls{yolo} detection is 42.8\,\gls{fps} on \gls{cpu}-only hardware, demonstrating that every module other than the detector operates well within real-time constraints even without \gls{gpu} acceleration. This means the system can be deployed on embedded \gls{gpu} platforms where the \gls{yolo} bottleneck is resolved by \gls{cuda} acceleration while all other modules run comfortably in parallel, with no pipeline stalling from any non-detection stage.

\textbf{Inference 5 — \gls{deepocsort}'s \gls{reid}-augmented tracking provides a decisive identity-preservation advantage.}
The identity switch count of 8 across the test sequences is dramatically lower than \gls{sort} (184 \gls{ids}, $22.9\times$ more switches), \gls{deepsort} (98 \gls{ids}, $12.3\times$), and \gls{bytetrack} (61 \gls{ids}, $7.6\times$). Identity switches are particularly damaging in maritime surveillance because each new track \gls{id} resets the \gls{lstm} history buffer, causing the predictor to fall back to kinematic mode and losing all accumulated behavioural context for that vessel. Every identity switch therefore introduces a temporary blind spot in trajectory prediction and collision risk assessment. The 8-switch result confirms that \gls{osnet} \gls{reid} embeddings provide sufficient discriminative signal to maintain vessel identity across the short-duration occlusions and camera wash-out events common in maritime video.

\textbf{Inference 6 — Multi-modal \gls{ais} fusion is a force multiplier for the entire system.}
The integration of \gls{ais} data does not merely add a data source — it fundamentally changes the system's operating characteristics across all modules. For detection, \gls{ais} provides a ground-truth anchor for matching camera-detected vessels, enabling dark vessel identification. For prediction, it enables the \gls{lstm} skip optimization that reduces prediction latency by up to 95.4\%. For collision detection, \gls{ais}-reported \gls{sog} and \gls{cog} provide more precise velocity vectors than pixel-space centroid tracking alone, reducing false alert rates. For weather robustness, \gls{ais} maintains vessel position continuity during camera visibility loss. No prior work reviewed in Chapter~2 simultaneously exploits all four of these \gls{ais} integration benefits.

The weather robustness evaluation further confirms that the proposed system maintains operationally useful performance even under moderate environmental degradation, and that the \gls{ais} fusion layer provides meaningful compensation for camera-based detection failures during severe visibility conditions.
